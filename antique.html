Antique I Abyss of Time

As it’s my first time to visit here, it seems it’s a place I have been familiar with from the abyss of a thousand years, and another thousand years. And suddenly, there’s a feeling that I was born to get here, and to finish some tasks. It’s certainly a beautiful country! But it’s a bad luck that my landlord has no other chamber available for me. But I implored that just let me stay here, even in the loft, and she agreed. It’s an old wooden hostel from the eras of antique, the days of yore, and a thick layer of dust cloaked the floor. I can’t help sneezing after I get into it, and seems silver needles dancing under the slanting moonlight which streams in from the tiny window. I just wandered around without any certain purpose, with a candle ignited in my hand.

The tolling of the bell, which witnessed the night’s soundness, from somewhere so far, far away. I can hear some liquid was trickling from somewhere, with a viscous feeling. A sporadic wail echoes in the distance. I remembered that I was lying on the timber, and gusty wind flooded over my ears, and as my palms crossed on my chest, my finger closed on the fingers of a stiff, ice-cold hand. And there’s blood-red rain coming down to my bed, stained my face. With the meow of cats, a most melancholy voice sobbed “unchain my soul”; I tried to draw back my palm, but the little frozen palm just clung to it, the voice’s shivering “Finally and in the end, you’re back.” Thereat began an almost screaming voice “Let me out, let me out. I’ve been imprisoned here for millennia.” I tried to calm myself down “Who are you? What can I do?”

“You got the key, almost, almost!” she cried, buried in that leather trunk. “Got the key!” The voice came out from all directions. “Got the key!” Terror made me clap my hands over my ears and close my eyes. Then it’s a deathlike silence after a continuous moaning.

I struggled to open my eyes, and see an eerie dim of light come upstairs, with the oak floor cracking, my blood almost clotted, and thank god, it’s the landlord with a lantern. The dim lantern light reflected in her eyes, like flames ignited. At last, she said in a half-whisper way “Why you opened that leather trunk?” But it seems she didn’t expect any answer, she hesitated, and lapsed into silence. A little black cat's meow ultimately broke the tension.

“What are you doing? Lie your body down as before, and until another morning. But never scream like that, for god’s sake!” Then she murmured to herself while leaving this little chamber.

I tried to look back at the leather trunk, what’s covering it is a cravat, with long uneven stitches on it, and you can see how flustered the manufacturer was when weaving this cravat, as I delved deep inside, a dull glow caught my eyes. I stretched my fingers towards it, and it’s a bunch of keys, perhaps from a very ancient time, the rust stains are everywhere on it. After a long scratch of my fingers, the rust scatteredly dropped. But at the thought of that mourning voice, all traces of drowsiness cleared up, and I decided to get dressed to go for some walk.

The night’s clear, like love can be, a wisp of bagpipe music wafting in it. It’s a very familiar melody but I have never heard it before. It’s a feeling like standing on short springy turf near the bank of the river, on a summer’s day, when the slanting rays of sun gilded the ground.

A delicate sugary scent hangs in the air, and the dim trees swaying faintly in the breeze, with its leaves stirring in dense masses like a woman’s hair. And I just stare deep into the limpid, crystal-like river water, until the moonlight gave it a face, it merged with another, another more youthful face. He raised his head and was calling somebody, and another face of a young girl appeared in the water, her eyes piercing the abyss, are so unforgettable. And she reached out her hands to the surface and cupped her hands and scooped up a little water, which made the two faces distorted in the water, and then the two faces vanished into the muffled thunder of hooves.

Antique II Hand Organ

Secretly were these words uttered—I clutched that bunch of keys in my hand; cold, hard, unyielding. Yet I dared not pursue nor satisfy my curiosity beneath my landlord’s roof. But what if I could become a cat? Cats know all things, their grim stares piercing through centuries, slicing through slander and lies. They merely witness, for some fleeting moments, then only comment with a single meow. I wished to be a white cat. But a caterwaul of cats tore me back to the present.

Still, I stood by the riverbank, on springy turf, beneath the sullen moonlight. A pair of small green eyes glinted—eerie emerald, a smoldering gloom in the dark, like embers fading to ash.

That unforgettable gaze was bequeathed from her forebears—older, perhaps, than memory itself. For hundreds, for thousands of years, those little green eyes had been cradled in a princess’s arms, and laid beside a plebeian’s hearth. Always there, to witness, to listen. Her eternal stare held fast, unblinking, etched in the abyss of time; she did not meow, only lifted her head to steal a glimpse, then crept a few steps and looked back—guiding my way.

The jungle was impenetrable; all light seemed swallowed whole. Wind bored into my bones through unseen crevices, thick with the aroma of trampled grass and loam. She ran faster and faster, and so did I. It felt as though I had sprinted for centuries, yet never grew weary. I felt myself growing younger, stronger, with every stride.

I thought dawn must be breaking somewhere, yet impenetrable darkness clung still—deathly silence, save for the jingle and clink of keys in my hand. For a moment, she looked back again. I knew at last where she meant to lead me: right ahead, in the jungle’s deepest heart, lay the wreckage of an ancient manor.

Domination and glory, and empires of a thousand years. All things repeat themselves, yet apocalypse lurks at every breath. No one can guarantee they’ll see the next minute; all fortune is a fleeting gift. Nothing truly belongs to us—and there is nothing she dares not do. The firmament was cloaked, a gloomy net stretched taut, impenetrable. She has bestowed so much hope upon us, so much aid—yet we know only the melody of our own lives, and have never heard another’s tune.

Somehow, it felt like standing on a rock in a river’s center: no way back, no way out. Eyes blindfolded, we read that book, yet not a word do we understand. Its pages turn, and burn. But what else do we care about? We are blind, not deaf—still, we can listen to her melody and dance to our delight, unburdened.

To this very melody, through a million years, she danced for those who could never see, and sang for those who could never hear—following its rhyme, unyielding. I can still hear the cries: the roar of crowds, the din of ancient wars. The lives of others—their apple trees, their fleeting days—pass by, fading, fading into this very moment.
